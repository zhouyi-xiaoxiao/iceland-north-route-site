Slide 01 / 20 - BioPath

Map in. Traps out. Proof attached.

Hi, I'm Xiaoxiao Zhouyi. BioPath is my ECEF project: a tool that turns a site map into better trap locations. The promise is simple: map in, traps out, proof attached. In the next few minutes, I want to show why that matters.

Slide 02 / 20 - The Strawberry Reason

This started close to home.

One reason this matters to me is personal. My grandfather's home grew strawberries, so crop protection was never only an abstract market or a line in a model. It was real: plants, labour, timing, and decisions made under uncertainty.

39 words
Slide 03 / 20 - The Problem

Trap placement is still too often a judgement call.

In pest control, trap placement often depends on local experience. That experience is valuable, but it can be hard to explain to someone else, or repeat on a new site. BioPath is not replacing judgement. It gives judgement something clearer to stand on.

43 words
Slide 04 / 20 - Why It Matters

A few metres can change the work that follows.

Why does that matter? Because a small placement decision can create a lot of follow-up work. If traps are in weak positions, people spend time checking, moving, and defending them. I want BioPath to help make the first decision better.

41 words
Slide 05 / 20 - Research Origin

Movement is uncertain. The environment shapes it.

The research idea is simple enough to say without equations. Movement is uncertain, but it is not random in every direction. Walls, corridors, entrances, and risky zones all shape where encounters are likely. That is the piece BioPath uses.

39 words
Slide 06 / 20 - Translation

ECEF moved the model toward a user decision.

ECEF helped me stop treating this only as a model. A model can be clever and still not help anyone. The user question is sharper: can someone draw a map, choose a budget, and understand the recommended placements?

38 words
Slide 07 / 20 - Fellowship Frame

The fellowship made the idea visible.

The fellowship gave the idea a deadline and a room full of useful pressure. It made me test the parts I used to postpone: who needs this, what they would trust, and what the first version should prove.

38 words
Slide 08 / 20 - What BioPath Does

A simple workflow people can question.

The product version is intentionally plain. A user starts with a site map and a number of traps. BioPath returns a ranked plan plus a benchmark. The important thing is not the picture; it is whether the recommendation can be discussed.

Slide 09 / 20 - Prototype

The prototype makes the story concrete.

I have built enough of the prototype to make the idea tangible. There is a solver, a small API, a web demo, and saved run outputs. It is not polished software yet, but it is no longer just a sentence on a slide.

Slide 10 / 20 - Model Layer

The map makes the assumptions visible.

Before BioPath solves anything, it needs to know what the site looks like. Where can movement happen? Where are the barriers? Which areas matter most? I use a synthetic map here so the assumptions stay visible and discussable.

Slide 11 / 20 - Solve Layer

Coordinates, not just a picture.

Then the model searches for placements. I do not need to show every calculation in this talk. The point is the change in the decision: instead of saying these spots look right, we can ask why these spots, and how good are they?

Slide 12 / 20 - Early Proof

Early evidence turns the next test specific.

The numbers here are not the finish line. They are a starting point for the evidence a pilot would need. Capture score, distance, and robustness give me concrete things to ask about, instead of asking vaguely whether this looks good.

Slide 13 / 20 - Customer Discovery

The hard question is trust.

ECEF changed the question from, can I make the algorithm work, to, who would actually use this? That is a bigger question. It includes trust, workflow, price, evidence, and whether pest managers want a decision aid in the first place.

Slide 14 / 20 - Language Shift

I had to translate novelty into value.

I also had to change my language. In research, I might start with mean first-passage time. In a room like this, that starts too far in. I should start with the decision: where to place traps, and why.

Slide 15 / 20 - The Product Promise

Map in. Traps out. Proof attached.

That phrase is the product promise. Map in means a real site. Traps out means a concrete recommendation. Proof attached means the decision can be questioned. If people remember that, BioPath has a story that can travel.
